Why is SMS 2FA insecure?

Structural weaknesses compared to TOTP and FIDO2

SIM swap attack
High risk

An attacker calls your mobile carrier, impersonates you using personal data bought from data brokers, and convinces them to transfer your phone number to a SIM card the attacker controls. Your phone loses signal. All your SMS codes now go to the attacker.

No malware needed — just a phone call to your carrier.
SS7 protocol exploit
Infrastructure flaw

SS7 is the 1970s-era signalling protocol that routes SMS worldwide. It was designed for trust between carriers, not security. Attackers with access to the SS7 network (nation-states, criminal groups, rogue carriers) can intercept and redirect SMS messages in transit — invisibly, without touching your phone.

No fix possible — it is a flaw baked into the global telecom infrastructure.
Real-time phishing interception
Common

A fake login page captures your password, then immediately logs into the real site and triggers an SMS. The fake page shows you a "please enter your code" prompt. You type the SMS code in — the attacker relays it to the real site within the 30-second window. Done.

TOTP apps are equally vulnerable to this attack.
Malware on device
Moderate

Android malware can read SMS messages directly from the inbox, including 2FA codes. Banking trojans have done this for years. The code arrives, the malware forwards it before you even see the notification.

Requires mobile signal
Availability

Unlike TOTP or hardware keys, SMS requires your phone to have mobile signal. Travel abroad, roaming issues, carrier outages, or simply poor signal can lock you out entirely.

Bottom line: SMS 2FA is much better than no 2FA — it stops most automated attacks. But it has structural weaknesses that TOTP and FIDO2 simply don't have. For any high-value account (AWS, email, banking), upgrade away from SMS. That is why AWS does not offer SMS as an option for root accounts at all.
